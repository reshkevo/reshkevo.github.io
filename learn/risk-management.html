Position sizing: the decision that comes first

Most new traders decide how many lots to trade based on how confident they feel about a setup. Risk-first traders do the opposite: the size is calculated from the account and the stop-loss, before confidence enters the conversation at all.

Risk amount = Account balance × Risk % per trade
Position size (lots) = Risk amount ÷ (Stop-loss in pips × pip value per lot)

Worked example: a $1,000 account, risking 1% per trade, with a 25-pip stop-loss on EUR/USD ($10/pip per standard lot):

  • Risk amount = $1,000 × 1% = $10
  • Position size = $10 ÷ (25 × $10) = 0.04 standard lots (4 micro lots)

Notice what drives the size: the stop-loss distance and the risk percentage. Not a gut feeling, not "I really like this trade." Use the risk calculator on the homepage to run this for your own numbers before every entry.

The stop-loss is part of the trade, not an afterthought

A stop-loss is a predefined exit that closes the trade automatically if price moves against you to a certain level. Two practical rules:

  • Set it based on where the trade idea is proven wrong — a level on the chart, like beyond a recent swing high/low — not based on "how much money I'm willing to lose." Those are different questions; only the first tells you where the setup actually fails.
  • Size the position to fit that stop, using the formula above — never widen a stop to make a position "fit" your account. If the correct stop makes the position too small to feel exciting, that's information about the trade, not a reason to remove the stop.

Risk-reward ratio

Risk-reward compares what you stand to lose against what you stand to gain if the trade works: a stop 20 pips away and a target 60 pips away is a 1:3 risk-reward ratio. This matters because it changes how often you need to be right to be profitable overall.

Risk-reward ratioWin rate needed to break even
1:150%
1:233%
1:325%

This is exactly why traders with a favourable risk-reward ratio can be profitable while being wrong more often than they're right — the size of the wins compensates for the frequency of the losses.

Why the math punishes big losses so hard

Losses and the gains needed to recover from them are not symmetrical. This is the single most under-appreciated fact in trading:

Loss on accountGain required to recover
10%11%
25%33%
50%100%
75%300%
90%900%
The whole point of this lesson

A 50% drawdown doesn't need a 50% gain to recover — it needs a 100% gain, because you're now recovering from a smaller base. This is why capping risk per trade (commonly 1–2%) and capping total risk across all open trades at once matters more than any entry signal. A strategy with a real edge can still fail an account that risks too much per trade; a mediocre strategy with strict risk control usually survives long enough to improve.

Setting a risk ceiling, not just a per-trade rule

Beyond sizing each trade individually, most risk-first traders also cap:

  • Total open risk — the combined risk of every position open at once, so five simultaneous trades don't quietly add up to 10% of the account at risk.
  • Weekly or monthly loss limits — a fixed drawdown level (for example, 5% in a week) that triggers a pause, a review of what's going wrong, and a reset — rather than trading through a losing streak trying to "win it back."

What's next
