Building a trading plan

Seven lessons of mechanics, math and mindset only pay off if they're written down as rules you actually follow. This is where it all becomes one page you can look at before every trade.

⏰ 8 min read📚 Putting it together
Advertisement

Why a written plan beats a mental one

A trading plan is simply the rules from Lessons 01–07, written down in one place, so a decision under pressure becomes "check the plan" instead of "figure it out right now." A plan that only exists in your head bends the moment emotion shows up — which, per Lesson 05, is exactly the moment it needs to hold.

1. Define your setups

Write down, specifically, what has to be true on the chart before you'll even consider a trade. Vague ("looks like it might go up") isn't a rule; specific ("price closes above the 50 EMA on H4 with the daily trend also up, entering on a pullback to that EMA") is.

  • What timeframe(s) you're trading
  • What trend and structure conditions must be present (Lesson 03)
  • Which, if any, indicators confirm the setup (Lesson 06)
  • What invalidates the setup before you'd even enter

2. Write your risk rules in numbers

This section should read like a checklist, not a philosophy — pull the numbers straight from Lesson 04:

Risk per trade: ____ % of account (commonly 1–2%)
Max total open risk across all trades: ____ %
Max daily / weekly loss before I stop trading: ____ %
Minimum risk-reward ratio I'll take: 1 : ____

Fill those blanks in before your next trade, not during it. Use the risk calculator to convert your risk percentage into an actual lot size every time.

3. Build a repeatable routine

A simple pre-trade and post-trade routine removes a surprising number of live decisions:

WhenWhat you do
Before the sessionCheck the economic calendar (Lesson 07) for high-impact releases; note daily trend and key levels (Lesson 03)
Before each entryConfirm the setup matches your written rules; run the risk calculator; set the stop-loss first
After each tradeLog it in the journal (Lesson 05), regardless of outcome
WeeklyReview the journal: which setups worked, which didn't, any rule broken and why

4. A journal template you can start today

From Lesson 05 — the minimum worth recording for every trade:

  • Date, pair, direction (long/short)
  • Setup type and one-sentence reason for the entry
  • Entry, stop-loss, target, position size, % risked
  • Outcome, and honestly: did I follow my own plan?
Where this connects

That last question — did I follow my own plan — is the single most useful line in the journal. A losing trade taken according to plan is a normal cost of doing business (see the risk-reward math in Lesson 04). A winning trade taken by breaking the plan is a warning sign wearing a disguise.

5. Revise the plan, but not mid-trade

Plans should improve over time as your journal gives you real data on what works. The rule: changes to the plan happen during the weekly review, calm and deliberate, never in the middle of an open position. Mid-trade is for execution only — that's the whole lesson from Lesson 05 in one sentence.
