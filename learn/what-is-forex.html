What is forex, actually?

Before charts, before strategy, before any of it — you need to know what market you're standing in. Here's the mechanical picture.

⏰ 6 min read 📚 Foundations
Advertisement

The market with no building

The foreign exchange market, forex or FX for short, is where one currency is exchanged for another. There's no single exchange floor like the New York Stock Exchange — it's a decentralized, over-the-counter network of banks, brokers and trading platforms all connected electronically, running effectively 24 hours a day on weekdays.

By trading volume, it's the largest financial market in the world. Daily turnover is measured in the trillions of dollars, dwarfing global stock and bond markets combined. That scale matters practically: on the major pairs, there's almost always enough liquidity to enter and exit a position without your own trade moving the price.

Who's actually in the market

It helps to know who you're sharing the order book with:

  • Central banks — managing currency reserves and, at times, intervening to influence their own currency's value.
  • Commercial banks — the backbone of the market, trading for clients and for themselves.
  • Corporations — hedging currency risk from international trade (an importer paying suppliers in another currency, for example).
  • Institutional and hedge funds — trading currencies as an asset class, often with large size and long time horizons.
  • Retail traders — individuals, trading through a broker, usually on shorter timeframes and smaller size. This is where most of us reading this sit.

Retail traders are a small fraction of total volume. That's not discouraging — it just means the market isn't designed around us, so the responsibility for managing risk sensibly is entirely ours. No one is going to do it for you.

Currencies are always quoted in pairs

You can't buy "dollars" in isolation — you're always exchanging one currency for another, so every quote is a pair: a base currency and a quote currency.

EUR/USD = 1.0850
base = EUR · quote = USD · meaning: 1 euro = 1.0850 US dollars

If you buy EUR/USD, you're buying euros and simultaneously selling dollars — you profit if the euro strengthens against the dollar. If you sell EUR/USD, you're doing the reverse.

Majors, minors and exotics

CategoryExamplesTypical trait
MajorsEUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD, USD/CHFAlways include USD, highest liquidity, tightest spreads
Minors / crossesEUR/GBP, AUD/JPY, GBP/JPYNo USD leg, still liquid, slightly wider spreads
ExoticsUSD/TRY, USD/ZAR, USD/KESA major paired with an emerging-market currency; wider spreads, sharper moves

The market runs in sessions

Because it's decentralized across time zones, forex trading is often described in terms of four overlapping sessions, named after the financial centres that drive them:

  • Sydney — the session opens the trading week, generally quieter.
  • Tokyo — Asian session, active for JPY and Asia-Pacific pairs.
  • London — the busiest single session by volume; most European trading happens here.
  • New York — overlaps with the back half of London for a few hours, historically the highest-volume window of the day.
Why this matters

The London–New York overlap is typically when spreads are tightest and moves are cleanest on the majors. Trading the same pair at 3am your local Tokyo-session hours can feel completely different from trading it during that overlap — same chart, different personality.
