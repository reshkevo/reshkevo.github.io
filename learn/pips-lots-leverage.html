Pips, lots & leverage

The units that turn a price move on a screen into an actual dollar amount in your account. Get comfortable with these before you place a single trade.

⏰ 7 min read📚 Foundations
Advertisement

What a pip is

A pip (percentage in point) is the standard unit for measuring a price move in forex. For most pairs it's the fourth decimal place; for pairs involving the Japanese yen, it's the second decimal place.

EUR/USD moves from 1.0850 to 1.0860 → that's 10 pips
USD/JPY moves from 148.20 to 148.50 → that's 30 pips

Some brokers quote an extra "fractional pip" digit (a 5th decimal on most pairs, 3rd on JPY pairs) — that's usually called a pipette, one-tenth of a pip.

What a lot is

A lot is a standardized trade size — it's how the market measures how much currency you're actually buying or selling.

Lot typeUnits of base currencyApprox. value per pip (EUR/USD)
Standard lot100,000$10
Mini lot10,000$1
Micro lot1,000$0.10

That pip value is exactly why lot size, not "how many trades," is the real lever on your risk. A 20-pip stop-loss on 1 standard lot risks roughly $200. The same 20-pip stop on 1 micro lot risks roughly $2. Same setup, same chart — wildly different risk, purely because of size.

Where this connects

This is exactly the math behind the risk calculator on the homepage: you tell it your account, your risk per trade and your stop distance, and it works backward to the lot size that keeps your risk where you want it.

Leverage: borrowed size, not borrowed money you keep

Leverage lets you control a large position with a small amount of your own capital, expressed as a ratio like 1:50, 1:100 or 1:500. Your broker sets aside a portion of your account — the margin — to open the trade.

Margin required = Position size ÷ Leverage
Example: $100,000 position at 1:100 leverage = $1,000 margin required

Leverage doesn't change how many pips a currency moves. It changes how much of your own capital is tied up controlling that position — and therefore how large your profit or loss is relative to your account, in both directions equally.

A worked example

Say you open 1 standard lot of EUR/USD ($10/pip) with a $2,000 account:

  • A 20-pip favourable move: +$200, a 10% account gain.
  • A 20-pip adverse move: -$200, a 10% account loss.
  • A 100-pip adverse move without a stop-loss: -$1,000, half the account gone.
Why this is the lesson people skip

Leverage is often marketed purely as opportunity. Mechanically, it's neutral — it magnifies whatever happens, good or bad, at the same rate. High leverage with no stop-loss and an oversized lot is the single most common way retail accounts are wiped out. High leverage combined with a small, deliberately sized position is just efficient use of capital. The leverage number itself isn't the risk; the position size you choose to run at that leverage is.

Margin calls, briefly

If losses on open positions erode your account equity down toward the margin being used, your broker will issue a margin call asking you to add funds or reduce positions, and may automatically close trades ("stop-out") to protect both you and itself from a negative balance. This is a mechanical safety net, not a strategy — the goal of good risk management is to never get near it.
