Fundamental analysis & the economic calendar

Charts show what price did. Fundamentals are a big part of why. Here's the short list of releases that actually move currencies, and how to trade around them without recklessness.

⏰ 8 min read📚 Analysis
Advertisement

What actually moves a currency's value

A currency's value is, at heart, a reflection of demand for the economy behind it. Fundamental analysis looks at the economic data, policy decisions and events that shift that demand, rather than the price chart itself.

Three forces dominate the list:

  • Interest rates — higher rates tend to attract capital seeking yield, supporting the currency; lower rates tend to do the opposite.
  • Inflation — central banks generally raise rates to cool high inflation and cut rates to support a weak economy, so inflation data is watched closely as a preview of rate decisions.
  • Growth and employment — a strengthening economy tends to support its currency; a weakening one tends to pressure it.

Central banks are the main character

Central banks (the Federal Reserve, European Central Bank, Bank of England, Bank of Japan, and others) set the interest rate policy that most other fundamentals feed into. Their scheduled meetings and statements are consistently among the highest-impact events on the calendar — not just the rate decision itself, but the tone of the accompanying statement and press conference, which shapes expectations for future meetings.

A distinction worth keeping straight

Markets often move more on whether a decision matched expectations than on the decision itself. A rate hike that was already fully priced in can produce a muted reaction, while a "no change" that surprises the market can move price sharply. It's the surprise relative to expectations that matters, not the headline number in isolation.

Reading an economic calendar

An economic calendar lists scheduled data releases and events by date, time and currency, usually with an impact rating. A few of the releases worth knowing by name:

ReleaseWhat it signalsTypical impact
Interest rate decisionsCentral bank policy stanceHigh
CPI (inflation)Price pressure in the economyHigh
Non-farm payrolls (US) / employment dataLabour market healthHigh
GDPOverall economic growthMedium–High
PMI (manufacturing / services)Forward-looking business activity surveyMedium
Retail salesConsumer spending strengthMedium

Most calendars also show the forecast (what analysts expect) alongside the previous reading, and update with the actual figure the moment it's released. The gap between forecast and actual is usually what moves price in the first few minutes.

Trading around high-impact releases

High-impact releases can produce sudden volatility, wider spreads, and price gaps through levels that would normally act as support or resistance — conditions where a normal stop-loss can behave less predictably than usual.

A risk-first way to handle news

Many risk-first traders simply avoid opening new positions in the minutes immediately around a high-impact release, and are deliberate about position size on any trade held through one. This isn't about predicting the news correctly — it's the same principle from Lesson 04: the stop-loss and position size need to reflect the actual conditions the trade will face, and volatility around news is a condition, not an exception to plan around after the fact.

Beyond the calendar: broader sentiment

Longer-running fundamental themes — a sustained shift in a central bank's policy path, geopolitical developments, commodity price trends for commodity-linked currencies like AUD or CAD — tend to show up as the broader trend context Lesson 03 talks about, playing out over weeks or months rather than a single release. Day-to-day price action sits inside that bigger picture.
