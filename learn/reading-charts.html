Reading a chart

Before any strategy, you need the vocabulary to describe what price is doing. This is that vocabulary.

⏰ 8 min read📚 Foundations
Advertisement

Anatomy of a candlestick

A candlestick summarizes price action over one time period — one minute, one hour, one day, whatever timeframe you're viewing. Each candle shows four prices:

  • Open — the price when the period started
  • High — the highest price reached during the period
  • Low — the lowest price reached during the period
  • Close — the price when the period ended

The thick part is the body, spanning open to close. The thin lines above and below are wicks (or shadows), showing the high and low. A candle where the close is above the open is typically shown in one color (often green) — price rose over that period. A close below the open is shown in another color (often red) — price fell.

Reading the shape

A long body means strong, decisive movement in one direction over that period. Long wicks with a small body mean price was pushed one way and then rejected back — a tug of war that ended close to where it started.

Timeframes change the story, not the facts

The same currency pair looks different on different timeframes because you're compressing more or less time into each candle:

TimeframeEach candle representsCommon use
M1 – M151–15 minutesVery short-term entries, needs full attention
H1 – H41–4 hoursCommon for swing entries within a day or two
D11 dayDaily trend and key levels, widely used as a reference
W11 weekLong-term structure, slow-moving, high-conviction context

A practical habit: check a higher timeframe first (like D1) to understand the broader trend and key levels, then drop to a lower timeframe (like H1) to time an entry within that context. Trading a 5-minute chart with no idea what the daily chart looks like is trading half-blind.

Support and resistance

Support is a price level where buying pressure has previously stepped in and stopped a decline. Resistance is a level where selling pressure has previously stopped a rally. They're not exact lines — think of them as zones where price has reacted before, and may react again because other traders are watching the same levels.

A useful reframe: once resistance is broken decisively, it often becomes support on a pullback (and vice versa) — the level's significance doesn't disappear, its role flips.

Trend structure

Trend is really just a description of the pattern of highs and lows:

  • Uptrend — a sequence of higher highs and higher lows.
  • Downtrend — a sequence of lower highs and lower lows.
  • Range / sideways — price oscillating between a fairly consistent support and resistance, with no clear directional structure.
A grounding habit

Before looking at any entry signal, get in the habit of asking three questions: what's the trend on the higher timeframe, where's the nearest support and resistance, and does what I'm about to do agree or fight with those two answers? That alone filters out a large share of low-quality trades.
